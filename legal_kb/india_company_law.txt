INDIA COMPANY AND BUSINESS LAW KNOWLEDGE BASE
===============================================
Comprehensive reference for Companies Act 2013, business structures, MOUs, shareholder agreements, and startup law in India.

─────────────────────────────────────────
SECTION 1: BUSINESS STRUCTURES IN INDIA
─────────────────────────────────────────

1. TYPES OF BUSINESS ENTITIES IN INDIA
(a) Sole Proprietorship: Single owner, unlimited liability, no separate legal entity. Governed by applicable state laws.
(b) Partnership Firm: Two or more partners, governed by Indian Partnership Act 1932. Unlimited liability. Registered or unregistered.
(c) Limited Liability Partnership (LLP): Hybrid of company and partnership. Partners have limited liability. Governed by LLP Act 2008. Minimum 2 designated partners.
(d) Private Limited Company: Minimum 2 directors and 2 shareholders. Maximum 200 shareholders. Cannot invite public to subscribe shares. Governed by Companies Act 2013.
(e) Public Limited Company: Minimum 3 directors and 7 shareholders. Can invite public investment. Listed or unlisted. Governed by Companies Act 2013.
(f) One Person Company (OPC): Single member company introduced by Companies Act 2013. One director and one shareholder. Must convert to private limited if turnover exceeds Rs. 2 crore or paid-up capital exceeds Rs. 50 lakhs.
(g) Section 8 Company: Non-profit company promoting commerce, art, science, education, charity. Profits used only for objects. No dividend to members.

2. PRIVATE LIMITED COMPANY IN INDIA — KEY FEATURES
(a) Separate legal entity — can own property, sue and be sued in its own name.
(b) Limited liability — shareholders liable only up to unpaid share amount.
(c) Perpetual succession — death or departure of member does not dissolve company.
(d) Minimum capital: No minimum paid-up capital requirement (removed by Companies Amendment Act 2015).
(e) Incorporation: File SPICe+ form on MCA21 portal.
(f) Directors: Minimum 2, maximum 15. At least one director must be Indian resident.
(g) DIN (Director Identification Number): Required for all directors.
(h) DSC (Digital Signature Certificate): Required for filing documents.
(i) Annual compliances: Annual return (MGT-7), financial statements (AOC-4), board meetings, AGM.

─────────────────────────────────────────
SECTION 2: COMPANIES ACT, 2013 — KEY PROVISIONS
─────────────────────────────────────────

3. MEMORANDUM OF ASSOCIATION (MOA)
The MOA is the charter document of a company. It defines:
(a) Name clause: Company name ending in "Private Limited" or "Limited".
(b) Registered office clause: State of incorporation.
(c) Objects clause: Main objects and other objects of the company.
(d) Liability clause: Liability of members (limited by shares or guarantee).
(e) Capital clause: Authorized share capital.
Any act beyond the objects clause is ultra vires and void.

4. ARTICLES OF ASSOCIATION (AOA)
The AOA governs the internal management of the company:
(a) Appointment, powers, and removal of directors.
(b) Conduct of board meetings and general meetings.
(c) Voting rights of shareholders.
(d) Transfer and transmission of shares.
(e) Dividend declaration.
(f) Borrowing powers.
Table F of Companies Act 2013 provides model articles for companies limited by shares.

5. BOARD OF DIRECTORS — KEY PROVISIONS
(a) Minimum 2 directors (private company), 3 (public company).
(b) Independent directors: Public companies with paid-up capital Rs. 10 crore+, turnover Rs. 100 crore+, or outstanding loans Rs. 50 crore+ must have at least one-third independent directors.
(c) Woman director: Mandatory for certain classes of companies.
(d) Resident director: At least one director must have stayed in India for 182+ days in the previous calendar year.
(e) Board meetings: Minimum 4 meetings per year with maximum 120 days gap between two meetings.
(f) Quorum: One-third of total strength or 2 directors, whichever is higher.

6. SHAREHOLDER RIGHTS UNDER COMPANIES ACT 2013
(a) Right to receive dividend.
(b) Right to attend and vote in general meetings.
(c) Right to inspect statutory registers.
(d) Right to receive annual report.
(e) Right to apply to NCLT for oppression and mismanagement (Sections 241-242).
(f) Pre-emption rights on new share issuance (right of first refusal).
(g) Right to receive notice of general meetings.
(h) Minority rights: Minority shareholders (10%+ in a private company) can petition NCLT for protection.

7. RELATED PARTY TRANSACTIONS
(a) Transactions between a company and its directors, key managerial personnel, relatives, or entities in which they have interest.
(b) Require board approval and shareholder approval (for certain transactions).
(c) Must be at arm's length — on commercial terms.
(d) Disclosure required in board report and financial statements.
(e) Listed companies: Additional SEBI requirements.

SECTION 3: SHAREHOLDER AGREEMENTS IN INDIA
─────────────────────────────────────────

8. SHAREHOLDER AGREEMENT (SHA) IN INDIA
A SHA is a private contract between shareholders governing their relationship and rights in the company. Key provisions:
(a) Share transfer restrictions: Right of First Refusal (ROFR), Right of First Offer (ROFO), Tag-Along, Drag-Along.
(b) Anti-dilution: Protection against dilution in future funding rounds.
(c) Board composition: Investor's right to appoint directors.
(d) Affirmative rights (Reserved Matters): Investor approval required for key decisions.
(e) Information rights: Investor's right to receive financial information.
(f) Liquidation preference: Order of distribution on exit (sale or liquidation).
(g) Vesting of founder shares: Founder shares vest over 3-4 years to prevent founders from leaving early.
(h) Non-compete and non-solicitation during and after association.
(i) Exit mechanisms: IPO, strategic sale, buyback.
(j) Dispute resolution: Typically arbitration.

9. TERM SHEET IN INDIA
A term sheet is a non-binding document outlining the key terms of an investment or M&A transaction. Key provisions:
(a) Valuation: Pre-money and post-money valuation.
(b) Investment amount and instrument (equity, CCPS, CCDS, convertible note).
(c) Liquidation preference: 1x non-participating or participating.
(d) Anti-dilution: Broad-based weighted average or full ratchet.
(e) Board composition.
(f) Founder vesting.
(g) Exclusivity: Startup agrees not to negotiate with other investors during due diligence period.
(h) Conditions precedent to closing.
Binding provisions in term sheet: Exclusivity, confidentiality, governing law.

10. COMPULSORILY CONVERTIBLE PREFERENCE SHARES (CCPS) IN INDIA
CCPS are preference shares that mandatorily convert into equity shares after a specified period or on occurrence of an event. Widely used by foreign investors investing in Indian startups under FDI regulations:
(a) CCPS are treated as equity for FDI purposes (RBI/FEMA regulations).
(b) They carry preferential rights on dividends and liquidation.
(c) Anti-dilution protection is embedded in the conversion ratio.
(d) Conversion price and ratio are specified at the time of issuance.

SECTION 4: MOU AND AGREEMENTS IN INDIA
─────────────────────────────────────────

11. MEMORANDUM OF UNDERSTANDING (MOU) IN INDIA
An MOU is a document expressing a mutual agreement between parties to work together. Key aspects:
(a) Binding vs. Non-binding: An MOU may or may not be legally binding depending on its language. If it contains consideration and clear intention to create legal relations, it may be binding.
(b) Common use: Joint ventures, partnerships, business collaborations, government agreements.
(c) Typical provisions: Purpose, scope of collaboration, responsibilities of each party, confidentiality, exclusivity, term, governing law.
(d) Limitation: MOUs are often used when parties are not ready to commit to a full contract — they serve as a framework for future negotiations.
(e) Enforceability: Courts look at the intention of parties — if MOU is meant to be binding, it will be enforced as a contract.

12. LETTER OF INTENT (LOI) IN INDIA
Similar to an MOU, an LOI expresses intention to enter into a formal agreement. Typically used in M&A, real estate, and business transactions:
(a) Usually non-binding (except exclusivity and confidentiality clauses).
(b) Sets out key commercial terms for negotiation.
(c) Creates moral obligation but not legal obligation (unless binding language is used).

13. JOINT VENTURE AGREEMENTS IN INDIA
A JV agreement governs the collaboration between two or more parties for a specific business purpose:
(a) Structure: Incorporated JV (new company) or unincorporated JV (contractual arrangement).
(b) Equity split: Ownership proportions of each party.
(c) Management: Board composition, voting rights, reserved matters.
(d) Profit and loss sharing.
(e) Capital contribution obligations.
(f) Exit: Buy-sell mechanisms (shot-gun clause, put and call options).
(g) Non-compete during and after JV.
(h) Dispute resolution: Arbitration preferred for JV disputes.

SECTION 5: STARTUP INDIA AND FOREIGN INVESTMENT
─────────────────────────────────────────

14. STARTUP INDIA — LEGAL FRAMEWORK
The Startup India initiative (launched 2016) provides benefits to recognized startups:
(a) DPIIT recognition: Apply at startupindia.gov.in — eligible entities: private limited company, LLP, partnership firm incorporated in last 10 years with turnover not exceeding Rs. 100 crore.
(b) Tax benefits: 3-year income tax exemption in the first 10 years, angel tax exemption (Section 56(2)(viib) exemption for recognized startups).
(c) IPR support: Fast-track patent examination, rebate on fees.
(d) Compliance benefits: Self-certification under 9 labour and 3 environment laws.
(e) Fund of Funds: SIDBI-managed fund investing in SEBI-registered AIFs that invest in startups.

15. FOREIGN DIRECT INVESTMENT (FDI) IN INDIA
FDI in India is regulated by FEMA (Foreign Exchange Management Act, 1999) and RBI:
(a) Automatic route: No prior government approval required — most sectors.
(b) Government approval route: Prior approval required for certain sectors (defence, media, insurance).
(c) Prohibited sectors: Lottery, gambling, chit funds, Nidhi companies, real estate (with exceptions), manufacturing of tobacco products.
(d) FDI reporting: Company must report FDI receipt to RBI within 30 days (FC-GPR filing).
(e) Downstream investment: Indian company with foreign investment investing in another Indian company — restrictions apply.
(f) Transfer pricing: Transactions between related parties (parent-subsidiary) must be at arm's length — Transfer Pricing regulations under Income Tax Act.

16. ANGEL TAX IN INDIA
Section 56(2)(viib) of Income Tax Act: If a closely held company issues shares at a premium above fair market value to a resident Indian investor, the excess is taxable as "income from other sources" in the hands of the company. This is called "angel tax."
(a) DPIIT-recognized startups are exempt from angel tax (as of 2023, extended to foreign investors too).
(b) Fair market value is determined by a Merchant Banker's valuation (DCF or NAV method).
(c) Angel tax has been a significant concern for Indian startups raising funds from resident investors.

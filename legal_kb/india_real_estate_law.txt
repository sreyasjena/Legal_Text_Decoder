INDIA REAL ESTATE LAW KNOWLEDGE BASE
======================================
Comprehensive reference for Indian real estate law, RERA, property transactions, and documentation.

─────────────────────────────────────────
SECTION 1: REAL ESTATE (REGULATION AND DEVELOPMENT) ACT, 2016 — RERA
─────────────────────────────────────────

1. RERA — OVERVIEW
The Real Estate (Regulation and Development) Act, 2016 (RERA) was enacted to protect homebuyers and promote transparency in the real estate sector.
(a) Mandatory registration of real estate projects with state RERA authority before advertising or selling.
(b) Mandatory registration of real estate agents.
(c) Developers must deposit 70% of project funds in a dedicated escrow account.
(d) Strict timelines for project completion — penalty for delay.
(e) Disclosure obligations: Project details, layout plans, approvals, timelines must be disclosed.
(f) Defect liability: Developer liable for structural defects for 5 years after possession.
(g) Grievance redressal: Homebuyers can file complaints with RERA authority, Adjudicating Officer, or Appellate Tribunal.

2. STATE RERA AUTHORITIES IN INDIA
Each state has its own RERA authority:
(a) Karnataka RERA (K-RERA): rera.karnataka.gov.in
(b) MahaRERA (Maharashtra): maharerait.maharashtra.gov.in
(c) UP RERA: up-rera.in
(d) Haryana RERA: haryanarera.gov.in
(e) Tamil Nadu RERA: tnrera.in
(f) Delhi RERA: rera.delhi.gov.in
(g) Gujarat RERA: gujrera.gujarat.gov.in
Homebuyers should always verify project registration on the state RERA website before booking.

3. RIGHTS OF HOMEBUYERS UNDER RERA
(a) Right to information: Access to project details, approvals, timelines.
(b) Right to possession: Developer must give possession on promised date or pay interest at SBI MCLR + 2%.
(c) Right to compensation: For false promises, structural defects, delay.
(d) Right to refund: With interest if developer fails to deliver or homebuyer wishes to withdraw due to developer's fault.
(e) Right to form Resident Welfare Association (RWA) after majority possession.
(f) Right to maintenance services until RWA is formed.

4. OBLIGATIONS OF DEVELOPERS UNDER RERA
(a) Register project before advertising or selling.
(b) Deposit 70% of collections in escrow account — use only for that project.
(c) Update project progress on RERA website quarterly.
(d) Complete project on time — any extension requires homebuyer consent.
(e) Hand over common areas to RWA within 3 months of majority possession.
(f) Obtain Completion Certificate and Occupancy Certificate before offering possession.
(g) Maintain project for 5 years after possession — rectify structural defects free of cost.

SECTION 2: PROPERTY PURCHASE AND SALE IN INDIA
─────────────────────────────────────────

5. AGREEMENT TO SELL VS SALE DEED IN INDIA
(a) Agreement to Sell: A preliminary document recording the intent to sell property on agreed terms and conditions. It does not transfer ownership. It is an executory contract — property transfers in the future.
(b) Sale Deed: The final document that actually transfers ownership of property from seller to buyer. It must be registered with the Sub-Registrar. Upon registration, ownership passes to buyer.
(c) Difference: Agreement to sell creates a right in personam (personal right); sale deed creates a right in rem (right over property).

6. ESSENTIAL DOCUMENTS FOR PROPERTY PURCHASE IN INDIA
(a) Sale Deed — main document of title.
(b) Encumbrance Certificate (EC) — shows all registered transactions on the property (mortgages, sale deeds). Obtained from Sub-Registrar office.
(c) Khata Certificate — municipal record of property ownership. Required for tax payment and building plan approval.
(d) Property Tax Receipts — proof of tax payment.
(e) Approved Building Plan — sanctioned by local authority (BBMP, BDA, etc.).
(f) Occupancy Certificate (OC) — issued by local authority confirming building meets approved plan.
(g) Completion Certificate (CC) — confirms construction is complete.
(h) Patta/Record of Rights (RTC) — for agricultural land.
(i) NOC from Society — for apartments in cooperative housing societies.
(j) Home Loan NOC — if property was under mortgage.

7. TITLE SEARCH AND DUE DILIGENCE IN INDIA
Before purchasing property in India:
(a) Verify title chain for last 30 years (minimum) — ensure clear and marketable title.
(b) Check for encumbrances — mortgages, charges, litigation.
(c) Verify EC (Encumbrance Certificate) from Sub-Registrar.
(d) Check property tax payment status.
(e) Verify approved plan and deviations.
(f) Check for pending litigation in court records.
(g) Verify seller's identity and authority to sell.
(h) Check if property is agricultural land — conversion required for non-agricultural use.
(i) Verify RERA registration for under-construction properties.
(j) Check if property is in notified area — may have restrictions on sale.

8. STAMP DUTY AND REGISTRATION CHARGES IN INDIA
Stamp duty is payable on property transactions. Rates vary by state:
(a) Karnataka: Stamp duty 5% + surcharge 10% on stamp duty + CESS. Registration charge 1% of property value. Women buyers get 1% rebate on stamp duty.
(b) Maharashtra: Stamp duty 5% (Mumbai 6%). Women buyers 1% rebate. Registration 1%.
(c) Delhi: Stamp duty 6% for men, 4% for women. Registration 1%.
(d) Tamil Nadu: Stamp duty 7%. Registration 4%.
(e) UP: Stamp duty 7% for men, 6% for women. Registration 1%.
Stamp duty is calculated on the higher of: agreement value or circle rate (government guidance value).

9. POWER OF ATTORNEY (POA) IN PROPERTY TRANSACTIONS
(a) General Power of Attorney (GPA): Gives broad authority to the agent.
(b) Special Power of Attorney (SPA): Specific authority for a particular transaction.
(c) Registered POA: Must be registered for immovable property transactions.
(d) GPA-based property sales: Supreme Court in Suraj Lamp Industries case (2011) held that property cannot be transferred through GPA — only through registered sale deed. GPA-based sales are not valid transfers of title.
(e) POA is valid only during the lifetime of the principal — automatically revoked on death.

SECTION 3: BUILDER-BUYER AGREEMENTS IN INDIA
─────────────────────────────────────────

10. BUILDER-BUYER AGREEMENT IN INDIA
A builder-buyer agreement (also called Agreement for Sale of Apartment) is a contract between a developer and homebuyer for purchase of an under-construction apartment or villa.
(a) Must be registered if value exceeds applicable threshold.
(b) Under RERA, developer must use the standard agreement format prescribed by the state RERA authority.
(c) Key terms: property description, payment schedule, possession date, penalty for delay, specifications, common amenities, maintenance charges, force majeure.
(d) Payment schedule: Linked to construction milestones or time-based.
(e) Possession date: Developer must commit to a possession date. RERA penalizes delays.
(f) Cancellation: Conditions under which either party can cancel and refund terms.

11. RED FLAGS IN BUILDER-BUYER AGREEMENTS IN INDIA
(a) No RERA registration number — illegal to sell without registration.
(b) Vague possession date — "subject to approvals" without a specific date.
(c) Developer's right to change specifications without buyer's consent.
(d) Unfair cancellation clause — developer can cancel but buyer cannot.
(e) Super built-up area calculation — verify carpet area (RERA mandates disclosure in carpet area).
(f) Maintenance charges not capped.
(g) No penalty for delay or penalty less than the interest charged on buyer's payments.
(h) Force majeure clause that is too broad — covering ordinary construction delays.
(i) No defect liability clause.
(j) Buyer liable for all statutory charges without specification.

SECTION 4: HOME LOANS AND MORTGAGES IN INDIA
─────────────────────────────────────────

12. HOME LOAN IN INDIA
(a) Equitable mortgage: Created by depositing title documents with the lender (bank). Most common form of mortgage for home loans in India. Does not require registration.
(b) Registered mortgage: Requires registration — gives lender priority over equitable mortgage holders.
(c) Loan Against Property (LAP): Loan secured against existing property.
(d) SARFAESI Act, 2002: Banks can take possession of mortgaged property without court order if borrower defaults — after giving 60 days' notice.
(e) Loan sanction vs. disbursement: Loan is disbursed in tranches for under-construction properties linked to construction milestones.
(f) Pre-payment: Most home loans allow pre-payment without penalty (RBI guidelines for floating rate loans).

13. REVERSE MORTGAGE IN INDIA
Senior citizens (60+ years) can mortgage their self-occupied property to a bank and receive regular monthly payments. The loan is repaid from proceeds of sale of the property after death. Governed by National Housing Bank guidelines.

SECTION 5: INHERITANCE AND SUCCESSION IN INDIA
─────────────────────────────────────────

14. SUCCESSION LAWS IN INDIA
India has personal laws governing succession based on religion:
(a) Hindu Succession Act, 1956: Governs Hindus, Sikhs, Jains, Buddhists. Equal rights for daughters in ancestral property (2005 amendment). Testamentary succession by will.
(b) Indian Succession Act, 1925: Governs Christians, Parsis, and those who die without leaving a will (intestate succession for most communities).
(c) Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act, 1937: Governs Muslims. Fixed shares for heirs under Islamic law. No equal inheritance for daughters.
(d) Special Marriage Act, 1954: For inter-religious marriages — Indian Succession Act applies.

15. WILL IN INDIA
(a) A will is a legal declaration of a person's intention regarding disposal of property after death.
(b) Requirements: Testator must be of sound mind and 18+ years. Signed by testator and attested by two witnesses who must not be beneficiaries.
(c) Registration of will: Optional but highly recommended. A registered will is harder to challenge.
(d) Probate: Court certificate confirming validity of will — required in some states (West Bengal, Maharashtra) for immovable property.
(e) Intestate succession: If no will, property distributed as per applicable personal law.
(f) A will can be revoked or amended at any time before death.

16. GIFT DEED IN INDIA
(a) A gift deed is a document by which a person (donor) voluntarily transfers property to another (donee) without consideration.
(b) For immovable property: Gift deed must be registered (Section 123 Transfer of Property Act).
(c) Stamp duty on gift deed: Varies by state — typically same as sale deed in most states. Between blood relatives, some states offer reduced stamp duty.
(d) Gift to minor: Valid — guardian accepts on behalf of minor.
(e) Gift must be accepted during the lifetime of both donor and donee.
(f) Revocation: Generally irrevocable after acceptance unless specific revocation conditions are mentioned.

INDIA INTELLECTUAL PROPERTY AND CONSUMER LAW KNOWLEDGE BASE
=============================================================
Comprehensive reference for Indian IP law, data protection, and consumer rights.

─────────────────────────────────────────
SECTION 1: COPYRIGHT ACT, 1957
─────────────────────────────────────────

1. COPYRIGHT IN INDIA — OVERVIEW
The Copyright Act, 1957 protects original literary, dramatic, musical, artistic works, cinematograph films, and sound recordings.
(a) No registration required — copyright subsists automatically on creation.
(b) Registration is optional but creates a public record and helps in enforcement.
(c) Duration: Life of author + 60 years for literary, dramatic, musical, artistic works. 60 years from publication for films, sound recordings, photographs, government works.
(d) Copyright Office: Registrar of Copyrights, New Delhi.

2. WORKS PROTECTED UNDER COPYRIGHT ACT
(a) Literary works: Books, articles, computer programs, databases.
(b) Dramatic works: Scripts, plays, choreography.
(c) Musical works: Compositions (not recordings).
(d) Artistic works: Paintings, sculptures, photographs, architectural works.
(e) Cinematograph films: Motion pictures.
(f) Sound recordings: Audio recordings.
Computer software is protected as a literary work under the Copyright Act.

3. OWNERSHIP OF COPYRIGHT IN INDIA
(a) Author is the first owner of copyright.
(b) Works made in course of employment: Employer is the first owner (Section 17) for literary, dramatic, musical, artistic works and computer programs created by employees in the course of employment.
(c) Commissioned works: For photographs, paintings, portraits, engravings, films — the person commissioning the work is the first owner (unless otherwise agreed).
(d) Government works: Government is the first owner of works made under government direction.
(e) Works of joint authorship: Co-owned by all authors.

4. COPYRIGHT INFRINGEMENT AND REMEDIES IN INDIA
(a) Infringement: Unauthorized reproduction, distribution, public performance, adaptation of copyrighted work.
(b) Civil remedies: Injunction, damages (actual damages or account of profits), delivery up of infringing copies.
(c) Criminal remedies: Imprisonment up to 3 years and fine up to Rs. 2 lakhs for first offence.
(d) Anton Piller order (search and seizure without notice): Available in India for urgent IP protection.
(e) Fair dealing: Research, private study, criticism, review, reporting current events — permitted without permission.

SECTION 2: PATENTS ACT, 1970
─────────────────────────────────────────

5. PATENTS IN INDIA — OVERVIEW
The Patents Act, 1970 (amended 2005 to comply with TRIPS) governs patents in India.
(a) Patentable subject matter: New inventions involving an inventive step and capable of industrial application.
(b) Non-patentable: Mathematical methods, mental acts, business methods, computer programs per se, literary/artistic works, discoveries, traditional knowledge, plants/animals, methods of treatment, inventions contrary to public order or morality.
(c) Duration: 20 years from filing date.
(d) Types of patents: Product patent and process patent (India grants both since 2005).

6. PATENT FILING IN INDIA
(a) Indian Patent Office: Offices in Mumbai, Delhi, Chennai, Kolkata.
(b) Provisional application: Filed to establish priority date — 12 months to file complete specification.
(c) Complete application: Detailed specification with claims.
(d) Publication: Automatically published after 18 months.
(e) Examination: Request for examination must be filed within 48 months.
(f) PCT (Patent Cooperation Treaty): India is a member — allows filing in 150+ countries through a single application.
(g) Fast-track examination: Available for startups, MSMEs, applicants from countries offering reciprocal treatment.

SECTION 3: TRADE MARKS ACT, 1999
─────────────────────────────────────────

7. TRADE MARKS IN INDIA
The Trade Marks Act, 1999 governs registration and protection of trade marks.
(a) Trade mark: A mark (word, logo, symbol, shape, color, sound) that distinguishes goods/services of one person from those of others.
(b) Registration: Not mandatory but provides statutory protection and exclusive right to use.
(c) Duration: 10 years, renewable indefinitely.
(d) Classes: 45 classes (34 for goods, 11 for services) under Nice Classification.
(e) Controller General of Patents, Designs and Trade Marks: Administers the Trade Marks Registry.
(f) Common law protection (passing off): Even unregistered marks can be protected if sufficient reputation is established.

8. TRADE MARK INFRINGEMENT IN INDIA
(a) Infringement: Unauthorized use of a registered trade mark in the course of trade.
(b) Passing off: Misrepresentation that goods/services of one person are those of another — available for unregistered marks.
(c) Remedies: Injunction, damages/account of profits, delivery up of infringing goods.
(d) Well-known marks: Broader protection — cannot be used even for dissimilar goods/services.

SECTION 4: INFORMATION TECHNOLOGY ACT, 2000
─────────────────────────────────────────

9. IT ACT, 2000 — KEY PROVISIONS
(a) Legal recognition of electronic records and digital signatures.
(b) Section 43: Penalty for unauthorized access to computers, data theft, virus introduction.
(c) Section 66: Computer related offences — imprisonment up to 3 years and fine.
(d) Section 66A (struck down by Supreme Court in Shreya Singhal case 2015): Was used for online speech offences.
(e) Section 66C: Identity theft — using another person's electronic signature, password.
(f) Section 66D: Cheating by personation using computer resource.
(g) Section 67: Publishing obscene material in electronic form.
(h) Section 69: Government power to intercept, monitor, or decrypt information.
(i) Section 72: Breach of confidentiality and privacy by intermediaries.
(j) Section 79: Safe harbour for intermediaries (not liable for third-party content if they act as mere conduits and follow due diligence guidelines).

10. INTERMEDIARY GUIDELINES AND DIGITAL MEDIA ETHICS CODE, 2021
Rules issued under Section 79 of IT Act:
(a) Significant Social Media Intermediaries (SSMI — 5 million+ users): Must appoint Chief Compliance Officer, Nodal Contact Person, Grievance Officer (India-based).
(b) Traceability: SSMIs providing messaging services must identify originator of messages if required.
(c) Grievance redressal: Acknowledge complaints within 24 hours, resolve within 15 days.
(d) Monthly compliance report.
(e) OTT platforms and digital news media: Subject to self-regulation and government oversight under Part III of the Rules.

SECTION 5: DIGITAL PERSONAL DATA PROTECTION ACT, 2023
─────────────────────────────────────────

11. DPDP ACT, 2023 — OVERVIEW
The Digital Personal Data Protection Act, 2023 is India's first comprehensive data protection law.
(a) Applies to: Processing of digital personal data within India; processing outside India if offering goods/services to persons in India.
(b) Data Principal: The individual to whom personal data relates.
(c) Data Fiduciary: Entity that determines purpose and means of processing personal data.
(d) Data Processor: Entity that processes personal data on behalf of Data Fiduciary.
(e) Consent: Processing requires free, specific, informed, unconditional, and unambiguous consent.
(f) Consent Manager: Registered entity that enables data principals to give/withdraw consent.

12. RIGHTS OF DATA PRINCIPALS UNDER DPDP ACT
(a) Right to information: Know what data is processed and for what purpose.
(b) Right to correction and erasure: Request correction or deletion of personal data.
(c) Right to grievance redressal: Raise complaints with Data Fiduciary or Data Protection Board.
(d) Right to nominate: Nominate another person to exercise rights on death or incapacity.

13. OBLIGATIONS OF DATA FIDUCIARIES UNDER DPDP ACT
(a) Process data only for lawful purposes with consent or legitimate uses.
(b) Give notice to data principals before/at time of collecting consent.
(c) Maintain security safeguards.
(d) Notify Data Protection Board and affected individuals of personal data breach.
(e) Erase data when no longer needed or on withdrawal of consent.
(f) Significant Data Fiduciaries (designated by government): Additional obligations — Data Protection Impact Assessment, Data Auditor appointment, Data Localization (if required).
(g) Penalties: Up to Rs. 250 crore per instance (Rs. 550 crore for breach affecting children's data).

SECTION 6: CONSUMER PROTECTION ACT, 2019
─────────────────────────────────────────

14. CONSUMER PROTECTION ACT, 2019 — OVERVIEW
Replaced the Consumer Protection Act, 1986. Key changes:
(a) E-commerce included: Online transactions covered.
(b) Product liability: Manufacturers, sellers, and service providers liable for defective products.
(c) Mediation: Consumer mediation cells at District, State, National levels.
(d) Central Consumer Protection Authority (CCPA): Regulates unfair trade practices, misleading advertisements.
(e) Enhanced pecuniary jurisdiction: District Commission — up to Rs. 1 crore; State Commission — Rs. 1 crore to Rs. 10 crore; National Commission — above Rs. 10 crore.

15. WHO IS A CONSUMER UNDER INDIAN LAW
Section 2(7): A person who buys goods or avails services for consideration. Includes:
(a) Buyer of goods for personal use (not commercial resale).
(b) User of services (including online services, financial services, medical services, education).
(c) Online buyers: Explicitly included since 2019 Act.
Excluded: Persons who buy goods for resale or commercial purposes.

16. CONSUMER RIGHTS IN INDIA
(a) Right to safety: Protection against hazardous goods and services.
(b) Right to information: Truthful information about goods and services.
(c) Right to choose: Access to competitive goods and services at fair prices.
(d) Right to be heard: Representation in consumer forums.
(e) Right to redressal: Compensation for unfair trade practices.
(f) Right to consumer education: Awareness of rights.

17. UNFAIR TRADE PRACTICES AND MISLEADING ADVERTISEMENTS
(a) Unfair trade practice: False representation about quality, standard, quantity of goods/services.
(b) Misleading advertisement: Misleads consumers, makes false claims, is considered harmful.
(c) CCPA can: Issue directions to discontinue misleading ads, impose penalty up to Rs. 10 lakhs (Rs. 50 lakhs for repeated offences), order recall of dangerous goods.
(d) E-commerce rules 2020: E-commerce entities must display country of origin, seller details, and genuine consumer reviews. No fake reviews.

18. E-COMMERCE RULES, 2020 IN INDIA
(a) Marketplace e-commerce entity: Cannot directly or indirectly influence prices in a manner contrary to applicable laws.
(b) Inventory e-commerce entity: Bears product liability.
(c) Mandatory disclosures: Legal name, address, customer care details, GSTIN, return/refund/exchange policy.
(d) No preferential treatment to related sellers.
(e) Grievance officer: Mandatory appointment. Acknowledge complaint within 48 hours, resolve within 1 month.
(f) Country of origin: Must be displayed for all goods.
